Character Creation

This page will include a lot of the information you will need to know before you go about creating your first character for Dungeons & Dragons 5th Edition, as well as some general tips that will be helpful when it comes to creating a character backstory for an TTRPG setting.

1. General Character Idea

First thing you should do before diving into the mechanics of any TTRPG, is to decide what kind of a character you want to play. This will vary drastically from player to player, but this often involves coming up with the beginnings of a backstory that sounds interesting to you. It's important not to get too bogged down in all the details of the story in the early stages, but deciding the kind of story you want to tell might inform the decisions you make once you dive into the mechanical part of character creation.

2. Pick Your Class

Picking your class has a heavy impact on the type of character you'll end up playing during your campaign. Once you've come up with your beginning character idea, you should decide the style of gameplay you're looking for.

The simplest breakdown of the different class types you'll see are Front line combatants, Spellcasters, and Half casters. Front line combatants primarily focus on the use of weaponry to get up close and personal with their enemies. Spellcasters generally stay towards the back of the group either casting spells that buff and provide utility to their allies, or throwing damaging spells at their enemies, or a mix of both. Half casters are often a mix of the two, but tend to have a greater variation of playstyle.

Each class also has different stats that are important towards being able to play them successfully. Barbarians and Paladins rely on Strength to make their attacks hit hard, while a Wizard depends on their Intelligence to boost their spellcasting capabilities. The way you want to play your character might influence what class you want to pick based on their most commonly used ability score.

3. Pick Your Origin

The first part to picking your origin is picking the species you will play as. There is a large variety of different peoples that exist in the worlds of Dungeons & Dragons. Each species comes with the option to increase certain ability scores, which can be helpful to give your character a statistical push towards the class you want to play. In a lot of cases, you story might not heavily influence what your best choice for species is, but each species does come with their own benefits. Perhaps you want to pick a species that works with your backstory based on your experience coming from a cold mountain village. In that case, you might end up picking to play a Goliath or a Dwarf due to their experience living in the mountians.

The other part of picking your origin is your background. Your character's background typically defines your character's lifestyle. It influences things like your profession, field of study, and hobbies. Say you are playing a character who lives on the wrong side of the law. You might be influenced to pick the Criminal background because it improves your characters abilities to Deceive and move Stealthily.

4. Roll for Stats

Your character has six ability scores: Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma. Each of these stats typically lies somewhere in the range from 3 to 20. As mentioned previously, you'll want to prioritize your highest ability based on the class you are playing, and the species you pick will increase certain stats.

There are two popular methods for determining your character's stats. The first of which is commonly referred to as the Point-Buy method. At this time, this method is unavailable in our character creator. The basic concept of this method is that all six abilities start at 8, and you are allocated 27 points to raise or lower each stat as you see fit. No ability is allowed to be lower than 3 or higher than 18.

The other popular method is to roll for stats. In this method, you roll one ability at a time. Roll 4 six-sided dice, and remove the one that rolled the lowest. Add together the 3 remaining dice, and that total is one of your abilities. You can put that number into whichever ability you like. Afterwards, continue to roll 4 dice and remove the lowest roll for each of your abilities until all 6 abilities have a score assigned to them. With this method, it is not possible to get a stat that is lower than 3 or higher than 18.

5. Finish Your Backstory

Now that you have a solid idea of how your character will play in the game, you can finish thinking about your backstory. Things you might think about detailing in your backstory are your character's family, how their background plays a role in their life, and what drove them to adventure. While it's important to have a backstory for your character that you can work off of, you don't want to overwrite. You want to leave enough room for your Dungeon Master to be able to surprise you with new things that tie into your backstory. You also want to leave room for new details to naturally come up through your character's growth.
